From the Gunship to the Dark Side of the Moon and Back

When they say, “live by the game,” I’m someone who has been steeped in the music biz. My first DJ gig was when I was 12 years old mixing “Weird Al” Yankovic with Disney songs on cassette tapes. Soon after that came heavy metal and hip-hop, and history was made!
As with every palette, it starts small and grows from there. Beginning with parodies set me up for a wide variety of styles, and soon that expanded into beat matching. When I was in high school, I was in a local band, which is where I learned how to sing and play guitar. It was during these years that I discovered the magic of Pink Floyd and started developing my affinity for effects and remixes.
After high school, I started working for the Street Team for a rip-hop band and started studying audio engineering. I almost got a gig as a live engineer, but the opportunity came at the wrong time, so I opted to stay in school. Already having years of DJ experience, I started playing at local parties and venues.
Although, always more of a hobby, I started working on small projects to expand my assets and eventually started putting together custom mixes. By this point, I was working with a particular style of hip-hop, and I used that to expand into other areas.
After years of working behind the decks, I started working with computer software, and my work became mainly about effects. Since then, I’ve worked on expanding my technical skills while researching new techniques and hardware. I started playing the harmonica, which landed me some back-up gigs, but nothing too substantial.
These days I’m more focused on the overall design of my sound, so I know I’ll be returning once I have enough skill to do something spectacular. Going back to some of my earlier inspirations, the members of Pink Floyd studied architecture, which gave them great insight into structure. Always a fan of soundscapes, I’ve used my unique beat matching skills to combine dubstep with hard rock, and many more innovative sounds. I still have several theme albums built up simply by lyrics, and with a bit of “spin” (pun intended), they can all be used to make sounds that define music.
